---
title: "Writing Prompts That Work"
---

The prompt is the difference between an agent that impresses on a demo and one that actually converts on live calls. 

Everything the agent does — what it says, how it decides, when it pushes and when it backs off — comes from the prompt.

Four principles to keep in mind:

**Clarity. **Be explicit about the agent’s role and objective. If you are vague, the agent will be vague. ‘You are a friendly assistant’ produces generic output. ‘You are a recruitment consultant at AutoSkills UK calling candidates who registered interest in automotive roles to book a callback with a senior recruiter’ produces focused, usable output.

**Constraints. **Define what the agent should not do. Do not discuss pricing. Do not offer discounts. Do not schedule outside business hours. Do not engage in off-topic conversation. These boundaries prevent the most common failure modes.

**Direction. **Every section of the prompt should guide the conversation toward the outcome you want. If the goal is a booked appointment, every response the agent gives should move toward that booking — even the objection handlers.

**Control. **The more freedom you give the agent to improvise, the more it will hallucinate and go off-script. Provide a skeleton conversation flow. Give it specific lines to say at each stage. Tell it what to do when the contact says yes, when they say no, and when they say something unexpected. Tight scripts outperform loose ones every time.
