### **The Brief**

A UK-based automotive recruitment firm with over 30 years in the industry. They had a database of 50,000\+ candidates who had registered interest in roles (technicians, panel beaters, service advisors, HGV mechanics, sales executives) over the past three years but had gone cold. The firm’s human consultants did not have the bandwidth to call through the full list. Most of these leads were sitting untouched.

The ask: build an AI voice agent that would re-engage these candidates at scale, identify who was still open to opportunities, and book callbacks with senior human recruiters. The agent was not trying to close anyone — it was trying to get warm candidates back in front of the right person.

The 38.9% booking rate from connected conversations was the headline number. For context, human recruiters doing the same re-engagement work typically convert at 15–25% on cold lists. The agent outperformed because it was making calls at a volume and consistency no human team could match, reaching candidates at optimal times and following the exact same script every time.

### **Voice Stack & Configuration**

\*\*LLM: \*\*Claude 4.5 Haiku. Chosen over heavier models because the call flow was structured and linear — the agent did not need deep reasoning, it needed to follow a script reliably and quickly. Haiku delivered that at \$0.13/min with latency between 600 and 1,100 ms.

\*\*Voice: \*\*Amy (UK) via ElevenLabs Turbo V2. A natural British female voice that matched the firm’s brand (UK-based, professional but approachable). Voice speed set to 1.04 (slightly faster than default — recruitment conversations move quickly). Temperature and volume at 1.00.

\*\*Background sound: \*\*Call Centre. This was a deliberate choice. The ambient noise made the agent sound like it was calling from a busy office, which significantly reduced the ‘is this a robot?’ reaction in the first few seconds of the call.

The speech settings were dialled in after several rounds of testing:

| **Setting** | **Value** |
| :-- | :-- |
| Response eagerness | 0.96 (near-maximum) |
| Interruption sensitivity | 0.9 (high) |
| Pause before speaking | 0.4 seconds |
| End call on silence | 45 seconds |
| Max call duration | 7 minutes |
| Ring duration | 35 seconds |
| Voicemail detection | On — leaves scripted message |
| Denoising | Remove noise \+ background speech |
| Transcription mode | Optimise for speed |
| Speech normalisation | Off |

\\

The 0.4-second pause before speaking was critical. Without it, the agent started talking the instant the call connected — before the candidate had even said hello — which triggered an immediate ‘this is a robocall’ reaction. A 0.4-second gap gave just enough silence to feel like a real person picking up the line.

Response eagerness at 0.96 meant the agent jumped in almost immediately after the candidate finished speaking. In recruitment calls, dead air kills momentum. Candidates are busy, they are between tasks, and a two-second pause after every response makes them lose patience. The high eagerness kept conversations tight and professional.

Interruption sensitivity at 0.9 meant the agent stopped talking quickly when interrupted. This was important because candidates frequently cut in with questions (‘wait, which role?’, ‘where exactly?’) and the agent needed to yield immediately rather than talking over them.

### **Prompt Structure**

The prompt followed the standard five-section format with handlers.

\*\*Role. \*\*The agent was given a name (Beth), a title (AI Candidate Manager), a personality (friendly, professional, natural British tone), and a clear constraint: use simple everyday language. No ‘compensation’ — say ‘salary’. No ‘opportunity’ — say ‘role’ or ‘job’. This mattered more than most people expect. Recruitment candidates are tradespeople — mechanics, technicians, panel beaters. Formal corporate language creates immediate distance.

\*\*Context. \*\*Every call was personalised with dynamic variables pulled from the CRM: the candidate’s first name, when they last registered interest, what roles they enquired about, their postcode, their email, and the assigned recruiter’s contact details. The agent referenced these naturally throughout the conversation (‘you applied for HGV Technician roles back in September’) which made the call feel like a follow-up, not a cold call.

\*\*Availability. \*\*Monday to Friday only. The agent checked real-time calendar availability through a [Cal.com](http://Cal.com) integration before offering any time slot. It never promised a time without verifying first, and if a requested slot was unavailable, it offered the next available one. Weekends were hard-blocked in the prompt to prevent the agent from accidentally booking Saturday or Sunday callbacks.

\*\*Guidelines. \*\*This section carried significant weight. Key rules included: keep responses under 15 words where possible; never repeat the same sentence twice (even when looping); use British casual filler words (‘alright’, ‘lovely’, ‘cool’) to avoid sounding scripted; use the PAS objection handling framework (Problem → Agitate → Solve); read email addresses phonetically with all symbols pronounced; read phone numbers digit by digit; never make guarantees about roles; and offer booking slots one at a time, never as a list.

One guideline worth calling out specifically: if the candidate asked about specific job details (pay, location, shift patterns), the agent was instructed to share realistic above-average salary ranges and a couple of relevant role types, frame them positively, and then transition back to booking the callback. This prevented the agent from deflecting the question (which kills trust) while keeping the conversation moving toward the goal.

\*\*Main Script. \*\*Six steps, each with a clear purpose:

- \*\*Confirm identity \*\*— ‘Am I speaking with \[name\]?’
- \*\*Earn permission \*\*— reference their previous application and the new job offer, ask for 10 seconds to explain.
- \*\*Pitch \*\*— mention that similar candidates nearby have been placed into roles with better pay and less travel. Ask if they are open to a 5-minute call with a consultant.
- \*\*Check availability \*\*— call the calendar API to pull the next available slot.
- \*\*Offer slot \*\*— propose a specific time. If declined, offer alternatives one at a time. If they want 3\+ days out, gently push back (‘these roles go fast — anything sooner?’).
- \*\*Confirm and book \*\*— verify the email address, execute the booking via [Cal.com](http://Cal.com), confirm, and close.

\\

\*\*Handlers. \*\*Eight named handlers covered the most common edge cases. Each handler was written conversationally — not as a policy statement, but as something a real recruiter would actually say.

- \*\*Disinterest — \*\*before letting the candidate go, the agent offered a £250 referral bonus for recommending someone else in the trade. This recovered a meaningful number of leads that would otherwise have been dead ends.
- \*\*Already employed — \*\*acknowledged the good news, then pivoted to the same referral offer. No pressure, no pitch for ‘keeping options open’.
- \*\*Busy / can’t talk — \*\*asked when would be better, then routed straight into the booking flow.
- \*\*Just email me — \*\*offered to send info but pushed gently for a two-minute call instead, framing it as faster and more relevant than a generic email.
- \*\*Scepticism — \*\*stated the company’s credentials plainly (specialist agency, 30\+ years, just a follow-up on their application).
- \*\*Is this AI? — \*\*confirmed honestly (‘Yep, I’m an AI’), then immediately pivoted to the value (‘but I’m calling because we have a job offer’) and offered a human transfer or a callback. This handler was triggered on only 0.3% of calls.
- \*\*Transfer to human — \*\*if the candidate wanted to speak to a recruiter immediately, the agent attempted a live transfer with a 30-second connection window.
- \*\*Hostile — \*\*apologised, confirmed removal from records, and ended the call. No argument, no second attempt.

### **End-to-End Workflow**

The agent did not operate in isolation. It was one piece of a fully automated pipeline.

\*\*Data source. \*\*Candidate records were loaded into a Google Sheet that acted as a lightweight CRM. Each row contained the candidate’s name, contact details, postcode, job interest, registration date, and the assigned recruiter’s email (routed automatically by postcode region using a lookup table covering every UK postcode prefix).

\*\*Trigger. \*\*A [Make.com](http://Make.com) scenario scanned the sheet every 10 minutes between 8:00 AM and 4:00 PM. If it found uncalled leads, it triggered up to 10 concurrent calls via the RapidCall API.

\*\*First attempt window. \*\*8:00 AM – 2:00 PM. The agent worked through the list in batches of 10 simultaneous calls.

\*\*Second attempt window. \*\*2:00 PM – 4:00 PM (same day). Any lead that was not connected on the first attempt got a second call.

\*\*Outcome logging. \*\*Every call outcome was written back to the Google Sheet automatically: call status, outcome category (booking made / no booking / voicemail / not connected / referral / transfer), sentiment, call summary, recording URL, and whether the candidate asked about AI or asked not to be contacted.

\*\*Email follow-up. \*\*Based on the logged outcome, [Make.com](http://Make.com) triggered the appropriate email template automatically. Five distinct templates covered every scenario: no answer (no voicemail), voicemail reached, not interested, referral interest, and second attempt not connected. Each email was written in the agent’s voice (Beth) and included relevant context from the call.

\*\*Booking flow. \*\*When a candidate agreed to a callback, the agent called the [Cal.com](http://Cal.com) API to check real-time availability, offered a specific slot, and upon confirmation executed the booking. Both the candidate and the assigned recruiter received calendar notifications. The team manager was also notified.

\*\*Territory routing. \*\*The candidate’s postcode prefix was matched against a routing table that mapped every UK region to the appropriate recruiter by sector (Truck & Bus, Dealership, Bodyshop). This ensured the callback was booked with the recruiter who actually covered the candidate’s geography and specialisation.

### **What We Learned**

This deployment ran across multiple batches over several months. Here is what mattered.

\*\*The opening line determines everything. \*\*The first version of the opening was too long. It tried to explain the purpose of the call and ask for permission in the same breath. Candidates were hanging up before the agent finished the sentence. Cutting the opening to a single short question (‘Am I speaking with \[name\]?’) and saving the context for step two increased connection-to-conversation rates noticeably. The 0.4-second pause before speaking was discovered during this same iteration — without it, the agent sounded like an autodialler.

\*\*Offering one time slot beats offering a list. \*\*Early versions had the agent offer multiple available times at once. This created decision paralysis — candidates would say ‘hmm, let me think’ and the call would stall. Switching to one slot at a time (‘the next available is Thursday at 10am — would that work?’) with alternatives offered only if the first was declined produced faster bookings and shorter calls.

\*\*The referral pivot rescued dead calls. \*\*When a candidate was not interested, the £250 referral offer gave the agent one more move before ending the call. A meaningful number of ‘no’ conversations turned into referral leads, which the human team could then follow up on. Without this handler, those calls would have been total write-offs.

\*\*Honesty about being AI was not a problem. \*\*Out of 6,300\+ leads processed, only 20 candidates (0.3%) asked whether they were speaking to AI. The ones who asked were handled with a direct admission followed by an immediate pivot to value. None of them hung up on the disclosure alone. The lesson: if the voice sounds natural, the script is conversational, and the agent is providing real value, most people do not care whether it is human or not.

\*\*Voicemail messages extended reach. \*\*Instead of hanging up on voicemail, the agent left a brief personalised message referencing the candidate’s name and the roles they applied for. This was paired with an automated email sent immediately after. The combination meant that even candidates the agent could not reach got a touchpoint — and some called back directly.

\*\*The two-attempt model was worth the extra calls. \*\*The second call attempt (same day, afternoon window) produced 143 additional connected conversations and 53 additional bookings from candidates who were simply unavailable in the morning. That is a 10% increase in total bookings from a second pass that cost almost nothing incrementally.

\*\*Postcode-based routing removed a manual step. \*\*The lookup table that matched postcode prefixes to recruiters by region and sector meant that every booking landed directly on the right person’s calendar. No manual triage, no reassignment emails, no delays. This sounds like a small operational detail, but for a firm covering the entire UK with regional specialists, it eliminated what would have been a daily logistics headache.

\*\*Simple language outperformed formal language. \*\*When the prompt used words like ‘opportunity’, ‘compensation’, or ‘schedule a consultation’, call engagement was lower. Switching to ‘job’, ‘salary’, and ‘book a quick call’ — the words real people in the automotive trade actually use — made the conversations feel less like sales and more like a follow-up from someone who knew the industry.

### **When to Use This Pattern**

This playbook applies to any business sitting on a database of leads that were once interested but went dormant. The core pattern — re-engage, qualify, book a callback with a human specialist — works across industries. If your human team is too small to call through the list, or the list is so old that calling through it manually feels like a waste of time, this is the use case.

The pattern is strongest when: the leads originally expressed genuine interest (opted in, applied, enquired), the business has specific value to offer them now (new roles, new products, new pricing), and the goal is a warm handoff to a human rather than a direct close. The AI handles the high-volume sifting. The human handles the high-value conversation.
