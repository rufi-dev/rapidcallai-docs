## **The Brief**

A US-based consumer finance company specialising in personal installment loans. Licensed to operate across multiple US states, with headquarters in the southeastern United States. Their product is straightforward: small personal loans with predictable repayment terms — designed as a more responsible alternative to payday lending.

The company had a recurring operational problem. Every day, a portion of their active loan portfolio fell past due. These were not bad-faith borrowers — they were existing customers who had missed payments, often due to timing, forgetfulness, or temporary cash flow issues. The company needed to contact them quickly and consistently, verify their identity, inform them of the outstanding balance, and offer a clear path to resolution — either a secure payment link or a transfer to a senior agent.

The challenge was volume and cadence. The company’s internal system scanned the loan portfolio three times a day and pushed newly overdue contacts into a queue. On a typical day, that meant roughly 300 contacts per push, with the full pipeline running around 900 calls daily. Human agents could not keep up with the pace, and the nature of the calls — repetitive, compliance-sensitive, emotionally delicate — made them a poor fit for manual handling at scale.

The ask: build an AI voice agent that could call overdue borrowers autonomously, verify their identity through name and date of birth, share the past-due balance, and either send a secure payment link via email and SMS or transfer the caller to a senior team member. The agent needed to sound like a real person, follow strict compliance rules, and — critically — treat every borrower with respect. These were still customers, and the company wanted them to remain as such.

## **Voice Stack & Configuration**

**LLM:** GPT 4.1. Chosen for this deployment because the call flow was linear and rule-bound. The agent did not need deep reasoning or creative responses — it needed to follow a strict compliance script, verify identity, and route to one of three outcomes. GPT 4.1 handled this reliably at $0.15/min with latency between 1,195 and 1,725 ms.

**Voice:** Cimo via ElevenLabs Turbo V2. A warm, steady American female voice that matched the company’s brand — professional but not corporate, calm but not cold. The voice needed to carry empathy without sounding patronising, which is harder than it sounds when the subject of every call is money owed. Voice speed was set to 1.06 (slightly faster than default to keep calls efficient without feeling rushed). Temperature at 1.08 (a touch above neutral, adding subtle warmth). Volume at 1.00.

**Background sound:** Call Centre. Same reasoning as the recruitment playbook — ambient office noise signals legitimacy. When someone answers a call about their loan and hears call centre ambience, it registers subconsciously as “this is a real company calling from a real office.” Without it, the silence between sentences can feel uncanny.

The speech settings were tuned specifically for the debt collection context:

| **Setting** | **Value** |
| :-- | :-- |
| Response eagerness | 0.98 (near-maximum) |
| Interruption sensitivity | 0.81 (high) |
| Pause before speaking | 0.4 seconds |
| End call on silence | Default |
| Max call duration | Default |
| Voicemail detection | On — leaves scripted message or silent drop |
| Denoising | Remove noise + background speech |
| Transcription mode | Optimise for accuracy |
| Vocabulary specialisation | General |
| Speech normalisation | Off |
| Reminder message frequency | 35 seconds, 1 time |

Response eagerness at 0.98 was deliberate. In debt collection calls, silence after a borrower speaks is interpreted as hesitation or uncertainty — and uncertainty from the caller erodes trust. The agent needed to respond immediately, with the same pace and confidence a trained human agent would have. Dead air in this context does not feel respectful — it feels like the caller does not know what to say.

Interruption sensitivity at 0.81 was slightly lower than the recruitment playbook (0.9). The reasoning: in debt collection, borrowers sometimes need to be allowed to vent, and the agent needs to hold its line long enough to deliver important information before yielding. Too high and the agent gets cut off mid-sentence every time the borrower makes a sound. At 0.81, it still yields quickly to genuine interruptions but holds steady through background noise and reactive sounds.

Transcription mode was set to optimise for accuracy rather than speed. In identity verification calls, mishearing a date of birth means a failed verification and a lost conversation. The slight latency trade-off was worth the improved recognition.

## **Prompt Structure**

The prompt followed the standard five-section format with handlers, but with one addition at the very top: a voicemail block. This was the highest-priority rule in the entire prompt.

**Role.** The agent was named Rosie and introduced as an automated assistant helping the team at {{CompanyName}}. The persona was warm, calm, empathetic, and confident — never pressuring. The prompt specified natural speech patterns: filler words like “okay,” “just to confirm,” and “alright.” The constraint was clear: guide callers through verification and account status with zero assumptions, promises, or guarantees. Rosie was not a debt collector. She was a customer care agent delivering an account update.

**Context.** Every call was personalised with dynamic variables pulled from the company’s loan management system: customer name, date of birth (for verification only), loan number (only last four digits shared), total past-due balance, days past due, a secure payment link URL, and the voicemail status flag. These variables were injected into each call via the workflow, so the agent always knew exactly who it was speaking to and what to say.

**Guidelines.** The compliance rules here were significantly more restrictive than in the recruitment playbook. Key rules included: verify both name and full date of birth before sharing any account details; never read back or hint at the date of birth on file; only one secure email per call; never discuss off-topic items; use the client’s name no more than three times per conversation; never stack questions (ask one, wait, then continue); before any transfer, explain that the connection may take up to 30 seconds; and pronounce all contact details phonetically (@ = “at,” . = “dot,” amounts spoken naturally like “about twelve hundred dollars”).

**Main Script.** Seven steps, each with a clear gate:

| **Step** | **Purpose** |
| :-- | :-- |
| 1. Greeting | Confirm identity: “Am I speaking with [name]?” |
| 2. Permission to verify | Explain the call purpose, disclose AI status, ask to verify |
| 3. Identity verification | Request date of birth (month, day, year). Compare internally. Never hint at what is on file. |
| 4a. DOB mismatch | Allow one retry. If still wrong, offer transfer to senior agent or end call. |
| 4b. DOB match | Confirm and proceed. |
| 5. Share balance + offer options | State the past-due amount and loan number (last 4 digits). Offer two paths: secure email + SMS with payment link, or live transfer to senior agent. |
| 6. Execute chosen action | Send SMS, transfer call, or both — based on borrower’s preference. |
| 7. Wrap-up | Confirm the action, offer a reminder to check their email, and close warmly. |

**Handlers.** Eight named handlers covered the most common edge cases. Each was written in the same warm, non-confrontational tone as the main script.

| **Handler** | **Approach** |
| :-- | :-- |
| Wrong person | Apologise briefly, end call. No follow-up, no redirect. |
| Not interested / told to get lost | First attempt: acknowledge, mention the 60-second account update. Second decline: respect it, mention {{CompanyWebsite}}, end call. |
| Refuses date of birth | Explain it is for their protection. Offer transfer to a senior agent as an alternative. |
| Scam / legitimacy concern | Validate the concern (“that’s a fair question”). Clarify no bank details or payments are taken on this call. Point to {{CompanyWebsite}}. Offer transfer. |
| Balance dispute | Do not argue. Offer to send the secure email with account details in writing, or transfer to a senior agent who can investigate. |
| Frustration or anger | Acknowledge the emotion (“I hear you”). Do not escalate. Offer transfer if the borrower is still upset after acknowledgement. |
| Call back later | Respect the request. Mention the company website. End call. |
| How did you get my number? | Explain it was from the loan application. Offer to continue or end. |

One design decision worth calling out: the agent disclosed its AI status proactively in Step 2 (“just so you know, I’m an automated assistant helping the team at {{CompanyName}} today”). In the recruitment playbook, the agent only confirmed when asked. Here, proactive disclosure was a deliberate compliance choice. Debt-related calls are more sensitive, and surprising a borrower with an AI reveal after they have already shared their date of birth would feel like a breach of trust.

**Screening / IVR Handler.** A practical addition for high-volume outbound. Many US mobile numbers use call screening services or automated attendants. The agent was given a specific handler: if it detected an automated system, it identified itself, stated the purpose, and asked to be connected. If the borrower then came on the line, the agent seamlessly picked up from Step 1 of the main script without referencing the screening interaction. This prevented the agent from getting stuck in IVR loops or failing silently when a human did not answer immediately.

## **End-to-End Workflow**

The agent was one piece of a fully automated pipeline. Unlike the recruitment playbook, which used [Make.com](http://Make.com) for orchestration, this deployment ran on n8n — self-hosted locally, which meant essentially zero workflow execution cost.

**Data source.** The company’s loan management system pushed overdue contact records via API to a Google Sheet three times per day. Each push contained approximately 300 contacts with all necessary fields: lead ID, customer name, email, state, loan number, date of birth, phone number, total past-due balance, days past due, and a secure payment link URL. The Google Sheet acted as a lightweight CRM — easy to monitor, filter, and audit without touching the production database.

**Call cadence.** Three daily windows: morning (around 9:00 AM EST), midday (around 12:30–13:00 PM EST), and afternoon (around 3:30–4:00 PM EST). When the n8n workflow detected new contacts in the sheet, it began calling immediately and worked through the list non-stop until all contacts in that batch were processed. If a borrower was reached during any window, they were automatically excluded from subsequent attempts that day. If not contacted, they remained in the pipeline for the next window.

**Voicemail logic.** The workflow tracked whether a voicemail had been left for each contact. On the first unreached attempt, the agent left a brief voicemail. On subsequent attempts, the voicemailDropped flag was set to TRUE, and the agent hung up silently without leaving another message. This prevented voicemail flooding and kept the company’s number reputation clean.

**Outcome logging.** Every call outcome was written back to the Google Sheet automatically: call status, outcome category (link sent / declined / transfer / voicemail reached / max voicemail reached / not connected), agent name, call time, sentiment, email status, a call summary, and a recording URL. The sheet became the single source of truth for daily operations — managers could open it at any point and see exactly where every overdue account stood.

**State-based routing.** The company was licensed in multiple states, and each state had its own lending regulations. The workflow filtered contacts by state before dispatching calls, ensuring the agent only called borrowers in states where the company was authorised to operate. The state field was also logged with every call record for compliance audit purposes.

**Payment link delivery.** When a borrower chose to receive the secure email and SMS, the agent triggered a send_sms function that delivered the borrower’s unique payment link. The link pointed to the company’s own payment portal, pre-populated with the borrower’s lead ID. No sensitive financial data passed through the agent or the workflow — the link itself was the secure handoff.

## **What We Learned**

This deployment ran continuously for six weeks across multiple US states. Here is what mattered.

**The voicemail strategy was the most important operational decision.** Without controlled voicemail logic, the agent would have left three to five voicemails per borrower per day. That is how numbers get flagged as spam, how borrowers file complaints, and how a legitimate outreach program turns into a nuisance. The two-state voicemail flag (leave one message, then go silent on subsequent attempts) kept the company’s caller ID clean and its complaint rate at zero.

**Proactive AI disclosure built trust instead of breaking it.** The early concern was that disclosing AI status would cause borrowers to hang up. It did not. By disclosing upfront (“just so you know, I’m an automated assistant”) and immediately transitioning to the verification step, the agent framed itself as a tool of the company, not a replacement for human contact. Borrowers who wanted a human got one — the transfer option was always available. Borrowers who did not care about the AI just continued.

**85% neutral sentiment is the right target for debt collection.** Positive sentiment in this context would actually be suspicious. Nobody enjoys receiving a call about money they owe. Neutral means the agent delivered the information clearly, treated the borrower with respect, and did not trigger hostility. The 11% negative was almost entirely from borrowers who were frustrated with the debt itself, not with the agent’s handling of the call. That distinction matters.

**Offering two resolution paths reduced call friction.** The agent always gave the borrower a choice: receive a secure email with a payment link, or speak to a senior agent. This framing was important. It was not “pay now or else” — it was “here are two ways I can help you handle this.” The choice itself communicated respect for the borrower’s autonomy. Most borrowers chose the email option, which makes sense: it is lower pressure, gives them time to review privately, and does not require a live conversation about money.

**Accuracy-optimised transcription was worth the latency cost.** The agent needed to verify dates of birth. A misheard digit meant a failed verification, a wasted call, and a frustrated borrower. Switching from speed-optimised to accuracy-optimised transcription reduced verification failures noticeably. The slight increase in response latency (a fraction of a second) was invisible in conversation but the improvement in recognition accuracy was measurable.

**The Google Sheet as a mini-CRM was surprisingly effective.** For a deployment of this scale, you might expect to need a dedicated CRM or database. In practice, the Google Sheet did everything the operation needed: real-time tracking, daily reporting, filtering by state or outcome, audit trails, and easy access for managers. The combination of n8n pushing data in and the Retell webhook writing results back created a self-updating system that anyone on the team could monitor without technical knowledge.

**Three daily call windows outperformed a single batch.** Spreading calls across morning, midday, and afternoon windows increased the chances of reaching borrowers at different points in their day. Someone who was driving at 9 AM might be free at 1 PM. The incremental cost of the second and third passes was negligible, but the cumulative reach was meaningfully higher than a single-batch approach.

**The handler for balance disputes prevented escalation.** When a borrower disputed the balance, the agent did not argue, explain, or justify. It acknowledged the concern and offered two paths: written details via email, or a live transfer to someone who could investigate. This was the right call. An AI agent that tries to defend a balance figure against a frustrated borrower will lose every time. The agent’s job was to de-escalate and route, not to resolve.

## **When to Use This Pattern**

This playbook applies to any business that needs to contact existing customers about an overdue balance, missed payment, or account status update at scale. The core pattern — verify identity, share a balance, offer a resolution path — is universal across consumer finance, utilities, insurance, healthcare billing, and subscription services.

The pattern is strongest when: the calls are high-volume and repetitive (hundreds per day), compliance rules are strict and non-negotiable (identity verification, disclosure requirements, regulated language), the goal is not to collect payment on the call but to guide the borrower toward a resolution channel, and the human team is better deployed on the cases that require judgment, negotiation, or exception handling rather than the initial outreach.

The AI handles the high-volume sifting. The human handles the high-judgment conversations. The borrower gets contacted promptly, treated respectfully, and given a clear path forward every time.
